I am a research scientist in machine learning at Facebook. I got my Ph.D in physics with a minor in computer science at Caltech in 2019, advised by Professor Xie Chen. I primarily worked on condensed matter theory, which concerns with the quantum mechanical behavior of physical systems with an extremely large number of degrees of freedom and at extremely low temperatures. Besides physics, I also studied statistics, machine learning, and data mining and worked on their applications to problems in computer vision, natural language processing, and recommender systems. Some of the codes for my projects are available on GitHub.

Prior to my Ph.D., I got a B.Sc. in physics from the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology in 2013. I also spent two semesters at Northwestern University as an exchange student in 2012.

Research

Exactly solvable model for two-dimensional topological superconductors
Z. Wang, S.-Q. Ning, X. Chen, Phys. Rev. B 98, 094502 (2018). [PDF]

The question of whether the two-dimensional topological superconductor, discovered in free fermion systems, can be realized in an exactly solvable model has long been open as such a model necessarily involves strong interaction. We give an affirmative answer by explicitly constructing such a model and rigorously proving its desired symmetry and gap properties. Our construction paves the way for studying the effect of strong interaction — like many-body localization — in topological superconductors, resolves a long-standing puzzle regarding time-reversal symmetry in such systems, and can be generalized to construct new strongly interacting fermionic topological phases without a non-interacting limit.


Twisted gauge theories in three-dimensional Walker-Wang models
Z. Wang, X. Chen, Phys. Rev. B 95, 115142 (2017). [PDF]

The Dijkgraaf-Witten models and the Walker-Wang models are two different frameworks to systematically construct three-dimensional topological phases, based on the physical pictures of membrane condensation and loop condensation, respectively. Can the Walker-Wang models be used to describe discrete gauge theories with nontrivial loop braiding statistics, which are known to be realized in the Dijkgraaf-Witten models? In this paper, we give an affirmative answer to the question by presenting an explicit construction of the Walker-Wang models which realizes both the twisted and untwisted gauge theories with gauge group the Klein four-group. By relating the Walker-Wang construction to the Dijkgraaf-Witten construction, our result opens up a way to study twisted gauge theories with fermonic charges, and correspondingly strongly interacting fermionic symmetry protected topological phases and their surface states, through exactly solvable models.


Fermionic symmetry protected topological phases and cobordisms
A. Kapustin, R. Thorngren, A. Turzillo, Z. Wang, J. High Energy Phys. 12, 052 (2015). [PDF]

We propose that fermionic symmetry-protected topological phases can be classified using spin cobordism groups. We test this proposal for the case of time-reversal or internal Z2 symmetry. We find that cobordism classification correctly describes all known fermionic symmetry-protected topological phases in space dimension less or equal than three and also predicts that all such phases can be realized by free fermions. In higher dimensions we predict the existence of inherently interacting fermionic symmetry-protected topological phases.


Holographic entropy inequalities and gapped phases of matter
N. Bao, C. Cao, M. Walter, Z. Wang, J. High Energy Phys. 09, 203 (2015). [PDF]

We derive the set of constraints satisfied by the entanglement entropy in quantum many-body systems with an area law, i.e., systems with the property that the entanglement entropy of any of its subsystem scales as the area of the subsystem boundary. We find that one of the entropy inequalities generalizes the Kitaev-Preskill formula for the topological entanglement entropy, and characterizes the universal property of a two-dimensional quantum many-body system with a gapped spectrum. Finally, we propose a candidate linear inequality for general four party quantum states.


Comparing the reliability of networks by spectral analysis
Z. Wang, K. Y. Szeto, Eur. Phys. J. B 87, 234 (2014). [PDF]

We numerically study the reliability of networks of the twisted ring topology subject to random edge failures. We find a novel relation between the network reliability and the spectral gap of the adjacency matrix. We give an analytical arguement of the validity of the established relation by using the Cheeger constant which relates the topological property of a network to its spectrum. We generalize the results by rewiring the twisted rings, and check the validity of the results by numerical simulations on the IEEE57, the Erdős–Rényi, and the Small-World networks.

Projects

Chinese characters on posters [code]

Text detection and recognition is an important problem in computer vision. Here, we focus on the detection and recognition of Chinese characters on posters. This is a challenging problem because the number of categories of Chinese characters is large, their arrangement on posters are less regular than that in other written texts, and they come in a variety of fonts and artistic styles. We made an attempt at this problem by experimenting with and combining different character-based models on scene-text detection and recognition. We achieved a mAP of 71.0% with a model based on YOLOv2 and Inception-v4.


Shakespeare sonnet generator [code]

We implemented a hidden Markov model and an LSTM to generate Shakespeare sonnets. We did some visualizations of the hidden states with the wordcloud package. We used the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary in NLTK to incorporate rhyme in the poem generation. We also explored how combining texts from both the Shakespeare sonnets and the Spenser sonnets affects the generated poems.


Movie recommender system [code]

We trained a matrix factorization model on the Netflix challenge dataset and the MovieLens100k dataset. We achieved an RMSE of 0.8677 on the Netflix challenge dataset. We further did a PCA of the matrices learned from the MovieLens100k dataset and visualized and interpreted the first two principal components.


Amazon sentiment analysis

We implemented logistic regression, SVMs, random forests, and AdaBoost models to predict the sentiment of comments on Amazon.
